We respectfully submit the original research article entitled **"Not All Social Determinants Travel: A Prefectural Ecological Analysis of Unemployment Rate and Diabetes Prevalence in Japan"** for consideration for publication in *Primary Care Diabetes*.

**Why this paper is appropriate for *Primary Care Diabetes*:**  
Social determinants of health—including unemployment—are frequently cited as upstream drivers of diabetes risk. However, the extent to which findings from high-unemployment, lower-income settings translate to a country like Japan, which maintains one of the world's lowest unemployment rates (approximately 2.6%) and a universal health coverage system, has not been systematically examined at a national level. We directly address this gap using the Japanese National Database of Health Insurance Claims Open Data (NDB Open Data), providing prefecture-level evidence from all 47 Japanese prefectures.

**Key findings:**  
Our primary finding is a null result: regional unemployment rate showed virtually no ecological association with the proportion of residents with HbA1c ≥ 6.5% (Pearson r = −0.016, p = 0.916), and this null was robust across six sequential OLS model specifications, including HC3-robust standard errors and outlier-exclusion analyses. By contrast, per-capita prefectural income was a strong inverse predictor (r = −0.570, p < 0.001). These findings suggest that, in a low-unemployment environment with universal healthcare, income inequality—not employment status—may be the more clinically relevant social determinant to target in diabetes prevention strategies.

**Novelty and relevance to primary care:**  
This study addresses a methodologically important but underexamined question: are social determinants of diabetes universally transportable across different socioeconomic and health-system contexts? The null finding has practical implications for primary care clinicians in Japan, who may benefit more from income-based risk screening than employment-status screening when identifying patients at high risk for poor glycaemic control. The ecological design also highlights the importance of context-sensitivity in translating public health evidence across countries.

**Ethical and data considerations:**  
This study used only publicly available aggregate data (NDB Open Data, 10th edition), which do not contain individual patient information. Ethics committee approval was not required.
